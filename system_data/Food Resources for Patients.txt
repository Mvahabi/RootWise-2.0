“Food insecurity” is a lack of consistent access to enough food. Many
people experience food insecurity because of low income, lack of affordable
housing, being socially isolated, health problems, and rising medical costs.
Because having enough food is necessary for your health and wellbeing,
IFM has created a list of resources below to support you in having access to
enough food.
SNAP (Supplemental Nutrition Assistance Program)
How it works:
SNAP (Supplemental Nutrition Assistance Program)
How it works:
Monthly benefits to purchase food at grocery stores, farmers’ markets and
food suppliers accepting SNAP. Benefits are loaded onto a EBT card. The
average benefit is $29 per week per person.
Who can apply:
Gross Income of approximately 130% of federal poverty level (some states
accept higher incomes). There are restrictions on which non-citizens are
eligible.

Special Supplemental Nutrition Program for Women, Infants, and Children
(WIC)
How it works:
Food packages around $40/month per person (age dependent) that are
nutritionally tailored. These can be redeemed in grocery and food stores
accepting WIC. WIC also offers support for breastfeeding, nutrition,
immunization, as well as health referrals.
Who can apply:
¢ Income lower or equal to 185% of federal poverty level.

The Emergency Food Assistance Program (TEFAP) and The Emergency
Food Network
How it works:
Food banks participating in TEFAP distribute goods to pantries, food
shelves, soup kitchens, social service agencies, and faith-based groups.
Who can apply:
It depends on site requirements, as some require referrals.
Website: USDA Emergency Assistance Program
Local Food Banks
How it works:
Local food banks offer a variety of food support depending on the location,
including shelf-stable goods, prepared meals, and fresh produce.
Who can apply:
Most food banks and pantries do not have a requirement for who can
receive food.
Websites:
• Feeding America
• FoodPantries.org
